  "theory": {
    "overview": "Social Science is not a single subject but an integrated approach to studying human society. It combines History (the study of past events over time), Geography (the study of places and environments in space), Political Science/Civics (the study of power, governance, and rights), and Economics (the study of production, distribution, and consumption under scarcity). Together, they provide a holistic view of human behavior and societal structures.",
    "keyPoints": [
      "Social Science is an interdisciplinary field connecting time, space, power, and resources.",
      "History provides context through the past; Geography explains location and environment.",
      "Civics defines rights, duties, and governance; Economics analyzes choices under scarcity.",
      "Real-world problems require integrating insights from all these branches."
    ],
    "detailedContent": "Social Science is often misunderstood as a collection of isolated facts—dates to memorize for History, lines on maps for Geography, or laws to recite for Civics. However, at its core, Social Science is a method of inquiry. It asks 'Why?' and 'How?'.\n\nHistory teaches us that the present is shaped by the past. Without understanding historical context (e.g., colonialism, independence movements), we cannot fully grasp current political boundaries or social inequalities.\n\nGeography reminds us that human actions are constrained and enabled by physical environments. Where a city grows, how agriculture functions, and where resources lie are geographical questions that dictate economic possibilities and settlement patterns.\n\nPolitical Science (Civics) explores the structures of power. It asks who makes decisions, what rights citizens have, and how laws affect daily life. It is the framework within which history unfolds and economics operates.\n\nEconomics is often seen as just about money, but it is fundamentally about choice under scarcity. Every decision—from a government building a dam to an individual buying bread—involves trade-offs. Economics provides the tools to analyze these choices efficiently.\n\nThe power of Social Science lies in its integration. A problem like 'Water Scarcity' cannot be solved by one discipline alone. It requires historical data on usage, geographical analysis of rainfall and aquifers, civic laws for distribution rights, and economic models for cost-effective solutions."
