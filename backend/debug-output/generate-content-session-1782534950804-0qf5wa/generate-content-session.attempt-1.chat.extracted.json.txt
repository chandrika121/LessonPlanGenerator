    "detailedContent": "The cell is the smallest unit that can perform all life functions. Robert Hooke's observation of cork was pivotal because it revealed a structured organization in living matter, even though he only saw dead cell walls. Modern biology classifies cells into two types: Prokaryotic (e.g., bacteria) which lack a defined nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, and Eukaryotic (plants/animals) which possess a true nucleus containing DNA. The outer boundary of the cell is critical for survival. In all cells, the plasma membrane acts as a gatekeeper, allowing nutrients in and waste out while maintaining internal balance—a property known as selective permeability. However, plant cells have an additional layer outside the membrane called the cell wall, made primarily of cellulose. This non-living structure provides mechanical strength and shape, preventing plants from collapsing under their own weight or wind pressure. Animal cells lack this rigid wall, allowing for greater flexibility."
