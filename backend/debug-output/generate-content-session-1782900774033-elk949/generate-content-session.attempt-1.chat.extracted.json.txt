    "detailedContent": "Matter can be broken down into two main categories: Pure Substances and Mixtures. A pure substance has a fixed chemical composition and distinct properties (e.g., Gold is always Au). Elements cannot be decomposed further by physical or chemical means, while compounds are formed from elements in fixed ratios (e.g., NaCl). In contrast, mixtures combine substances physically without changing their identities; they can be separated by physical methods like filtration or distillation. Homogeneous mixtures appear uniform throughout (solutions), whereas heterogeneous mixtures have visible distinct parts.\n\nIn the laboratory, we measure matter using mass. It is crucial to distinguish this from weight, which varies with location (e.g., Earth vs Moon). Chemistry relies on mass because chemical reactions depend on the number of atoms/molecules present, not gravitational pull.\n\nTo communicate measurements accurately, scientists use Significant Figures. These are all certain digits plus one uncertain digit. Rules for counting them include ignoring leading zeros and including trailing zeros if a decimal point is present. When calculating with these numbers, the result's precision cannot exceed that of the least precise measurement used in the calculation."
